# The foundations: the IMPaCT cohort

The Precision Medicine Infrastructure associated with Science and Technology was established by the Carlos III Health Institute (ISCIII) in 2020. Its first program, Predictive Medicine, aims to develop a large population-based cohort in Spain (the IMPaCT cohort), recruiting 200,000 participants who will be prospectively followed over time. This initiative is aligned with other major international cohorts such as All of Us (USA), UK Biobank (UK), CONSTANCES (France), LifeGene (Sweden), and NAKO (Germany), and seeks to improve understanding of the causes of diseases and health conditions of major public health relevance, particularly chronic diseases.

A key component of the IMPaCT cohort is the assessment of lifestyle factors, especially physical activity. To address the limitations of traditional self-reported measures, expert groups led by David Martínez Gómez proposed the inclusion of enhanced questionnaires on physical activity and sedentary behavior, together with objective monitoring using wearable devices.

Participants will wear these devices continuously for seven days. The selected wearable incorporates multiple sensors, including a triaxial accelerometer that captures movement along three axes (x, y, z), environmental and body temperature sensors, and a photoplethysmograph providing heart rate and heart rate variability. Importantly, two devices will be worn simultaneously—one on the wrist and another on the thigh—allowing multi-location measurement. This setup enables the collection of nine biomechanical and physiological signals, significantly improving the accuracy of lifestyle and activity monitoring.

This approach places the IMPaCT cohort at the forefront of wearable-based health research, as no other large-scale cohort has implemented multi-location devices combined with such a range of signals. However, it also introduces significant analytical challenges, since each participant generates hundreds of millions of data points over the monitoring period. Processing these large-scale data will require advanced algorithms but will ultimately provide valuable insights into physical activity, sleep, and overall health-related behaviors.
